By this point, I’ve seen enough of what AI tools can do to appreciate both the excitement and the frustration. Sometimes they help you move fast and think wider. Other times, they feel like they’re taking you in circles. You get plenty of output, **but very little forward movement.**

That’s where I started to rethink *how I was working*.

At first, I treated AI like a sketchbook — something to fill with options and see what came out. I’d prompt ideas, generate visual concepts, draft headlines, explore voice. And often, I ended up with a folder full of things that looked good but didn’t feel ready or useful. There was no clear next step. Just more material.

The real shift came when I stopped asking the tools to figure things out for me. I began using them with more intention. Not just to generate, but to assist once I already had a sense of where I wanted to go.

---

# The Way I Work Now

These days, I start by **defining what I am actually trying to make**. A newsletter post? A brand concept? A page for a client? I write down what the outcome should look like — not in detailed specs, just something clear enough to hold onto. I try to think about the audience, the feeling I want to communicate, and the action I want to take.

Once that is set, I use prompting as a companion. Not the director of the project — just a helpful voice in the room. I might ask for a headline variation, or help finding a tone that feels more honest, or a way to explain something more clearly. Sometimes I compare a few drafts, not to copy any of them, but to spot what is missing from mine.

When I feel like I am going in the right direction, I move fast. If something feels clear enough to publish or send, I stop polishing and just get it out.

---

# What AI Is Good For (In My Case)

It helps unblock me when I’m stuck. It helps me write faster when I already know what I want to say. It helps me explore multiple versions of an idea so I can choose what feels right. And sometimes, it helps me realize what doesn’t need to be written at all.

But the key difference now is that I use it **after** I’ve done the thinking — not before. I don’t sit down hoping the AI will give me direction. I bring the direction, and the tool helps me move.

---

# The Work Is Still Yours

Finishing things is still the hard part. That hasn’t changed.

What has changed is that I have more momentum. I’m not stuck on drafts anymore. I know when something feels ready enough. And I know that if I want to make better use of AI, the work starts long before the prompt.

I no longer feel like I’m testing a tool. **I’m just working. With help.**
