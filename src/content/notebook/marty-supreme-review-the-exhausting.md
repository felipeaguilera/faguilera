# Synopsis

In Marty Supreme, Josh Safdie returns to his natural habitat: the story of a man chasing something that the world considers completely worthless. Marty Mauser (Timothée Chalamet) works in a shoe store in 1950s New York City. His real life, the one that matters to him, happens on the ping pong table. He is convinced, deeply and stubbornly convinced, that he can become the greatest table tennis player in America. Possibly in the world.

What follows is a relentless journey across different social worlds. Marty scrapes together money to compete in London. He collides with a wealthy social circle, including a fading actress turned socialite (Gwyneth Paltrow), people who fund ambitions they don’t fully understand. He builds his career against a Japanese champion named Endo, turning what should be a niche sport into his own personal Cold War. He damages every relationship around him in the process and barely notices.

The premise sounds absurd when you describe it out loud. Timothée Chalamet plays the guy who plays ping pong. That’s the movie. But in Safdie’s hands, the absurdity is the point. Marty Mauser is loosely based on a real person, Marty Reisman, an actual American table tennis champion of the era. The reality that someone lived this life, believed this deeply in something the world dismissed, makes the film stranger and more fascinating than any fiction could.

# What I Liked

**The Safdie Intensity:** From the first scene, this film has the same electric charge as *Uncut Gems* or *Good Time*. There is no neutral gear. Every scene feels like something is about to go wrong, even when nothing is. The sound design and Daniel Lopatin’s score, which draws from 1970s and 80s textures despite the 50s setting, create a constant, low-level anxiety. You feel Marty’s pressure and intensity over his actions.

**Kevin O’Leary Playing Kevin O’Leary.** The casting of the real-life businessman as a shark entrepreneur who hovers around Marty’s career is a genuinely brilliant move. He is supportive and antagonistic at the same time, the kind of person who believes in you only insofar as believing in you benefits them. It is oddly one of the most realistic performances in the film, precisely because he is not acting.

**The Ping Pong as High Stakes.** At a certain point in the film, when Marty faces his Japanese rival, Endo. It stops feeling like a sport and starts feeling like Rocky versus Ivan Drago. You know you should not care this much about a ping pong match. You care anyway. The match scenes are impressive, fun and amazing. Props to Safdie’s on making you feel the gravity of something quite small.

# What Could be Better

**Chalamet Commits, But Doesn’t Explode.** He is good. He is physically committed. The accent, the movement, the intensity are very good. But I didn’t have a moment where I forgot it was Timothée Chalamet. With the Safdie brothers’ best performances like *Robert Pattinson* in *Good Time*, you lose the actor inside the character. That didn’t fully happen here for me. Good performance from a good actor, but it doesn’t feel like we met “Marty” too deeply.

**The Runtime Doesn’t Earn Its Weight.** At two and a half hours, the film occasionally feels like it is following Marty’s rhythm too closely. His life has no pauses, and neither does the edit. Maybe if this film was twenty minutes shorter, it might lose nothing of importance and gains a lot of breathing room to reflect more.

# My Insight

Marty Supreme is a film about the specific loneliness of being focused and determined in the wrong place. Marty is not deluded. He actually is that good. The tragedy is that he is excellent at something that 1950s America has decided doesn’t matter, and his entire personality has been shaped around forcing the world to care. The conviction and the cost of it is real. The people around him pay that cost: his partner, his family, the people who try to help him. He doesn’t notice, or can’t afford to.

I think we won’t remember this film the way we remember *Uncut Gems*. It doesn’t have that film’s suffocating dread or its perfect structural trap. But it has something else: the portrait of someone betting everything on a version of themselves that the world doesn’t want yet. That feeling is very specific. And Safdie captures it with a kind of love and clear-eyed sadness at the same time.

# Rating🌟8/10

## *An exciting and deeply rooted American film about the price of being absolutely certain you are right.*

***Recommendation:** Go in knowing it’s a Safdie film, not a sports movie.*

# For Discussion

Marty burns through everyone who tries to support him because his conviction in his own dream leaves no room for anything else. Is that kind of total obsession a character flaw, or is it the only way some things ever get achieved? Specially in this small niches?

# Related Works

- **Uncut Gems (2019)** — The closest thing in Safdie’s own catalog. Same pulse, same doomed obsession, same feeling that disaster is one bad decision away.
- **Good Time (2017)** — For the raw, street-level Safdie energy that Marty Supreme refines but never fully replaces.
- **Anora (2024)** — For another portrait of someone whose conviction outpaces the reality around them, and who pays for it.
- **Rocky (1976)** — The obvious reference, and an honest one. The underdog facing a culturally loaded opponent is the same emotional engine.
- **The Wrestler (2008)** — For the specific loneliness of someone who is extraordinary at something the world has moved on from.
