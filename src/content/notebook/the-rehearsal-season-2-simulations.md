# **Synopsis**

Nathan Fielder returns with a second season of *The Rehearsal*, a show that blurs the line between documentary, performance, and social experiment. This time, he moves beyond helping awkward people prepare for tough conversations. The focus shifts toward aviation, specifically how miscommunication in cockpits can lead to real disasters.

Through obsessive recreations of black box data, cockpit simulations, and even the life story of Captain “Sully” Sullenberger, Nathan explores how hierarchy and hesitation can kill, and whether rehearsing extreme scenarios can make humans act better when it matters most.

# **What I Liked**

- **A bold theme shift:** Going from trivia team confessions to aviation safety is unexpected but powerful. It turns the show into something more than just awkward comedy.
- **Total commitment:** Nathan builds cockpits, hires experts, simulates bonding lounges for pilots. He is all in, and it shows.
- **Sincerity under the weird:** There is a strange but real intention behind the absurdity. He is not joking around — even when it looks like he is.
- **The Sully experiment:** Rehearsing an entire person’s life just to understand one moment. Ridiculous, moving, unforgettable.
- **You want Nathan to win:** You end up rooting for Nathan, not just to pull off the show, but to really improve something in the world.

# **What Could Be Better**

- **Some bits go too far:** There is a puppet breastfeeding scene crosses the line from surreal to just uncomfortable for the sake of it. But that’s Nathan style. Be warned.
- **Emotional detachment:** Nathan is hard to read. His deadpan delivery makes it tough to know what is genuine. And that can be engaging for someone like me, but clearly some people might have issues with his persona.
- **Unsettling Reality:** Even that i know It is part of the concept but not knowing what is real for too long can become unsettling.

# **My Insight**

What makes *The Rehearsal* stand out is not just the concept. It’s the intention. Nathan is not mocking people. He is not interested in being edgy or clever for its own sake. What he is doing, even when it is absurd, is rooted in a deep curiosity about how we behave under pressure, how we fail each other, and whether we can change that.

Season 2 does not just ask “What if you could prepare for life?” It asks: “Can preparation prevent disaster?” When Nathan tries to pitch ideas to real congresspeople, when he pushes to directly affect flight training culture, you realize this is not just content. It is a mission, or at least a serious attempt to be useful in a very unconventional way.

It is uncomfortable and weird. But in a media landscape full of predictable formulas, *The Rehearsal* feels like a necessary anomaly. Something that takes creative risk seriously, even when it looks completely ridiculous.

# **For Discussion**

*There is something strange and fascinating about watching a show that does not just explore a topic — it tries to intervene in it. The Rehearsal Season 2 raises a bigger question: Can a piece of television go beyond commentary and make the world safer, even in a tiny way?*

*Nathan starts by simulating conversations. Now he is simulating life-or-death decisions inside cockpits. But he is not parodying pilots or mocking aviation training, he is genuinely wondering whether our systems of communication, authority, and decision-making are broken. And if they are, can absurd rehearsal lead to real insight?*

*This opens a deeper territory. What happens when comedy stops trying to entertain and starts trying to solve something? And more importantly, how far can that go before it becomes either delusional or brilliant — or both?*

*The discomfort in the show often comes from not knowing what’s performance and what is not. But I suppose that is the point too. A lot of our own activities in life are rehearsals in a way. We prepare, repeat, avoid, hesitate. Nathan just literalizes that in a studio set.*

*So, the real question is: Are we watching a comedian lose his grip on reality? Or someone using television to reflect how fragile reality already is? I think is the latter.*

# **Related Series or Movies**

- ***Nathan For You*** (2013–2017) – Created by Nathan Fielder  
  The origin of Fielder’s deadpan, reality-bending style. Each episode follows a business owner who receives absurdly elaborate advice from Nathan. Uncomfortable, clever, and one of a kind.
- ***The Curse*** (2023– ) – Created by Nathan Fielder and Benny Safdie  
  A fictional dark comedy series that extends Fielder’s fascination with awkwardness and failure into a scripted format. Disturbing and slow but shares thematic DNA with *The Rehearsal*.
- ***American Vandal*** (2017–2018) – Created by Dan Perrault and Tony Yacenda  
  A true-crime parody that unexpectedly evolves into a sharp, emotional look at teenage life and systems of feeling. More grounded but equally committed to its premise.
- ***Cunk on Earth*** (2022– ) – Created by Charlie Brooker  
  A mockumentary series following Philomena Cunk, a clueless presenter who explores human history with absurd sincerity. Shares *The Rehearsal*’s tonal tension between factual information and surreal comedy.
- ***The Act of Killing*** (2012) – Directed by Joshua Oppenheimer  
  A haunting documentary where former death squad leaders reenact their crimes as cinematic set pieces. Far darker, but eerily close in the way performance reveals hidden truths.
