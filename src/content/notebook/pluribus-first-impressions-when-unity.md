## **Synopsis**

Vince Gilligan returns with his first sci-fi series for Apple TV+. *Pluribus* follows a world where an alien code merges human minds into one global consciousness — eliminating crime, conflict, and loneliness. But a small group of immune dissidents, led by a novelist (Rhea Seehorn), refuses to join. The show isn’t about action. It’s about whether peace is worth the cost of individuality.

## **What I Liked**

- **Production at Scale** — The budget shows. The visual coordination of a synchronized world is stunning without being flashy. It’s less spectacle, more argument.
- **Patience as Strategy** — This is the slowest thing Gilligan has made. No cliffhangers. No quick cuts. It trusts you to stay with it, and that alone feels radical.
- **An Unbearable Protagonist —** Seehorn plays someone you might hate in real life. Stubborn. Self-righteous. But you understand why she won’t give in. That’s hard to pull off.
- **The Core Question —** What if everyone being happy meant no one was free? The premise doesn’t need twists. It just needs time to breathe.

## **What Could Be Better**

- The Pace Will Lose People — If you’re expecting *Breaking Bad* tension, this isn’t it. Some episodes feel like they’re testing your commitment.
- Platform Limits Its Reach — Apple TV+ isn’t Netflix. Fewer people will find this, and that’s a shame.

## **My Insight**

The closing credits say: *“This series was created by humans.”*

That line does more work than most of the dialogue. *Pluribus* is expensive, slow, and imperfect. It required hundreds of people, years of work, and a singular vision that could’ve gone wrong at any moment. It’s the opposite of a prompt.

The show itself mirrors its premise. It asks: do you want the efficient thing, or the human thing? The one that makes sense fast, or the one that lingers and changes shape the longer you think about it?

I think Gilligan is saying the mess is the point. That struggle, disagreement, and inefficiency aren’t bugs — they’re what make something worth making.

## **Rating: 🌟9/10: Bold and necessary, but not always easy to watch**

## **For Discussion**

In a world that rewards speed and consensus, what makes something worth the time it demands? And if we’re moving toward tools that deliver instant satisfaction, what happens to the art that asks you to wait?
