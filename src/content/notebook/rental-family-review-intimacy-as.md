# **Synopsis**

In *Rental Family*, director Hikari takes us into the quiet, high-pressure world of Tokyo to tell the story of a unsuccesful American actor (Brendan Fraser). Struggling with his career and the isolation of living in Japan, he takes a job at a unique agency: “Rental Family.” The company offers surrogate relationships. Clients can rent a father for a wedding, a best friend for a lonely afternoon, or a husband to satisfy family expectations.

Brendan Fraser’s character becomes a professional “family member,” stepping into the lives of strangers to help them navigate social pressures and emotional voids. It is a “dramedy” that moves between the absurdity of the premise and the deep, heartbreaking reality of the clients who need it.

# **What I Liked**

**The Cultural Context:** The movie does a brilliant job explaining *why* this service exists. As I learned from my partner who lived in Japan, there is a massive pressure to “contribute to society” and maintain harmony (”wa”). Japanese citizens often feel they “cannot afford to be on the brink.” They rent family members not to deceive others maliciously, but to avoid burdening the group with their own messiness. It’s a survival mechanism.

**The ‘School Interview’ Arc:** The most compelling storyline involves a single mother who rents Fraser to play the father for her daughter’s interview at a prestigious school. It captures the tension perfectly: the clumsiness of an American trying to fit a Japanese mold, combined with the genuine care he develops for the child. You find yourself rooting for the lie to succeed because the emotional stakes for the child are so real and pure.

**The Respect for Legacy**: There is a beautiful subplot involving a legendary, aging actor suffering from Alzheimer’s. His daughter hires a “fake interviewer” to document his life story, giving him a final sense of dignity and legacy. It reframes the “fake” service as an act of profound kindness. It respects the story of someone who has been forgotten by the culture.

## **What Could Be Better**

**The Ethical Ambiguity**: The movie walks a fine line. The owner of the agency is portrayed as a “good guy” trying to help, but the business creates complex, sometimes violent emotional situations. The film touches on the ethics of building relationships based on lies, but sometimes the “feel-good” tone glosses over how damaging these transactions could be for the people playing the roles.

**The Outsider American:** Brendan Fraser plays the “clumsy but good guy outsider” very well, but at times it feels like a familiar device to make the Japanese setting relatable for Western audiences. In my opinion, the movie is strongest when moves from Fraser “adaptation” and goes in depth into Japanese clients struggles and motivations.

## **My Insight**

Performance is a kindness. We often judge “fakeness” as a moral failure. *Rental Family* argues the opposite. In a society where “being on the brink” is judged, a rented father or a fake interviewer allows people to maintain their dignity. The characters aren’t renting people to trick the world; they are renting them to survive it. The movie suggests that sometimes, a performed connection is more compassionate than a brutal truth.

## **Rating 🌟9/10.**

### *A beautiful, calm, and emotionally intelligent look at how we outsource our loneliness.*

## **For Discussion**

We pay for therapists and caregivers. Is paying for a “friend” or “father” really that different, or does the introduction of money into personal intimacy cross a line? Where is that line, and how we can understand it in a society like the Japanese but possible it would be a complete disaster in west of the world?
